# Narrative Structure Analysis

Most writing advice treats a piece of writing as a container for information. This skill treats it
as a *construction* — a sequence of decisions the author made about what to say first, what to
withhold, what evidence to reach for, and how to keep the reader moving. The goal is to make those
decisions visible, the way an architect's floor plan makes visible the choices a finished building
hides.

The failure mode to avoid is producing a summary with extra headers. A summary answers "what does
this piece say." This analysis answers "why does it say things in this order, and what is it doing
to the reader at each step." If your output could be produced by someone who only read the piece
once and skimmed for main points, you haven't done the analysis — you've done a book report.

## Read the whole piece first, twice if it's short

Read start to finish before analyzing anything. The structural choices only become visible in
retrospect — a callback in the conclusion only reads as a callback once you've seen the opening it's
calling back to; a "turn" in the argument only reads as a turn once you know where the piece ends up.
Analyzing paragraph-by-paragraph as you go will make you miss the shape of the whole.

If given a URL, fetch and read the actual content, not a cached snippet or search-result excerpt.

## Produce a report with these four sections

Always use this structure. Each section answers a distinct question — resist the urge to blend them,
since "what they're saying" and "how they built it" are genuinely different axes and collapsing them
is what turns this into a summary.

### 1. Thesis — what is the author actually trying to say

State the single core claim or takeaway in one or two sentences, in your own words, not the
author's. Look past the author's own framing: writers often bury the real point under a narrative
hook, a personal anecdote, or academic hedging, and state something safer or more general in their
literal topic sentences. Ask "if a reader remembered exactly one idea from this a week later, what
would change how they think or act?" — that's the thesis, even if the piece never states it that
plainly.

Distinguish the thesis from the topic. "This piece is about remote work" is a topic. "Remote work
fails when companies port in-office rituals instead of redesigning for asynchrony" is a thesis.

### 2. Content Outline — how the narrative is built

Walk through the piece section by section (using the author's actual headers/breaks if present, or
natural paragraph clusters if not) and for each one, name two things briefly:
- **What it covers** — a short label, not a re-summary of the content
- **What job it's doing for the argument** — e.g. establishes stakes, introduces the counterexample,
  pivots the argument, complicates the initial claim, delivers the payoff, resolves the hook from the
  opening

The point of the second column is that sections in good writing rarely just "add information" — they
move the reader from one state of understanding to the next. A section that only restates the thesis
in different words is doing weaker work than one that complicates it, and that's worth calling out.

Keep this scannable — a list or lightweight table, not paragraphs. Something like:

```
1. Opening anecdote (a failed product launch) — hooks with a concrete failure, establishes stakes
2. "Everyone blames X" — states the conventional explanation, sets it up to be knocked down
3. The reframe — introduces the author's actual claim as a correction to #2
4. Three supporting cases — builds credibility through repetition/pattern, not just assertion
5. Return to the opening anecdote — reinterprets it through the new lens, closes the loop
```

### 3. Structural Pattern — the shape this piece follows

Name the overall pattern, e.g.: problem→solution, before/after, inverted pyramid (lead with the
conclusion, then justify), listicle, narrative arc (setup→conflict→resolution), thesis→antithesis→
synthesis, myth-busting (claim→common belief→debunk→truth), Trojan horse (draws you in with one
promise, delivers a different payload), or a hybrid of these. If none of the standard shapes fit
cleanly, describe the actual shape rather than forcing it into a named box — the goal is accuracy,
not vocabulary.

Note where the piece deviates from its own pattern — a listicle that suddenly develops a throughline
in item 7, an inverted pyramid that holds back one key fact for a late reveal. Deviations are usually
deliberate craft choices, not sloppiness, and are often the most instructive part of the analysis.

### 4. Rhetorical Strategy — how it tries to move the reader

Identify the main levers the author pulls to be convincing or compelling, for example:
- **Evidence type**: data/statistics, anecdotes, analogies, thought experiments, expert citation,
  the author's own lived experience — and whether it leans on one type almost exclusively
- **Appeals**: logic/mechanism (logos), credibility/expertise (ethos), emotion/identity (pathos) —
  which is doing the heavy lifting
- **Devices**: hooks, callbacks, repetition/rule-of-three, deliberate contrarianism, rhetorical
  questions, a recurring metaphor that gets extended through the piece
- **Where it's load-bearing vs. decorative**: does removing a given device weaken the argument, or is
  it just style?

Close with one or two sentences on where the construction is strongest and where it's thinnest —
e.g. a claim in the outline that's asserted but never actually backed by the evidence catalogued
above. This is the payoff of doing the analysis: it should let the reader see a seam in the
craftsmanship that a plain read-through wouldn't surface.

## Calibrate to what's actually there

Not every piece has all four dimensions in equal strength. A quick opinion post might have a thin
structural pattern and a thick rhetorical strategy; a reported feature might be the reverse. Say so
plainly rather than padding a weak section to match the others — "this section is mostly transitional
and doesn't add a distinct rhetorical move" is a more useful sentence than a forced paragraph.

If the source is short (a tweet-length post, a short memo), the four sections can be brief — a
sentence or two each is fine. The structure matters more than the length.
